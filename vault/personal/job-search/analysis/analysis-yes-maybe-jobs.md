### Theme 1: "Building" vs "Operating" Language

Simon consistently says YES to roles that use **building** language. The strongest emotional responses correlate with:

| Building language (YES trigger) | Operating language (lower excitement) |
|---|---|
| "Build from the ground up" (Coles) | "Maintain and support" |

| "Reimagine the in-store experience" (Coles) | "Monitor and maintain" |
| "Entering an exciting phase of transformation" (Tes) | "Business as usual" |
| "Newly created role" (Robert Walters) | "Existing team management" |

Simon's most enthusiastic notes (_"unbelievable!"_, _"incredible experience!"_, _"really exciting!"_) all appear on roles where the JD emphasises creating something new.

### Theme 2: AI as the Differentiator

Simon explicitly mentions AI as a reason for interest in **7 of 21 jobs** (33%). He frames AI in three distinct ways:

1. **AI as a product skill:** _"Having the understanding and technical skills to be able to implement and use AI within the product development life cycle"_ (Upper Echelon)
2. **AI as an operational transformation lever:** _"This area is ripe for change, especially with relation to how AI can manage, do root cause analysis, and actually fix things"_ (ConnectEast)
3. **AI as a domain knowledge equaliser:** _"AI is going to change how we look at this, and interpret and understand dynamics within a market"_ (Energy One)

This is not just a buzzword attraction. Simon has a thesis: AI changes what product managers and IT leaders need to know. He sees it as closing his domain knowledge gaps and amplifying his execution capability.

### Theme 3: The "Trusted Adviser" / Relationship Builder Identity

Across both tracks, Simon identifies as someone who builds relationships and earns trust:

- _"My superpower of getting to know customers and onboarding customers"_ (Hays)

This is his bridge between the two tracks. Whether in a product role or an IT leadership role, he sees himself as the person who deeply understands what people need and translates that into action.

## Part 4: Summary Findings

### What makes Simon say YES

1. **The role involves building something** — a new product, a new team, a new operating model, a new customer experience. Not maintaining something that already exists.

2. **The JD uses pragmatic, direct language** — "get stuff done," "bias to action," "player and coach," "pragmatic approach." Corporate-speak JDs with no substance get filtered out.

3. **There is an AI or transformation angle** — either explicitly (AI/ML product development) or as a latent opportunity (transforming IT operations via AI). Simon sees AI as his current competitive advantage.

4. **The company has a mission he can connect to personally** — education (his kids), farming (his friend), end-user experience (his passion), building products (his identity).

5. **The role bridges product and technology** — Simon is not purely a product manager and not purely an IT leader. His strongest responses come when the role description blurs those boundaries.

6. **He can see direct customer/user impact** — whether that is millions of Coles shoppers, school teachers and students, or farmers managing cattle. Roles where the impact is abstract or internal-only generate lower enthusiasm.

### What Simon is honest about

1. **Domain knowledge gaps** — energy, agriculture, data, construction. He compensates with his argument that AI reduces the domain expertise barrier.

2. **Specific technical depth** — full-stack development, SaaS activation/monetisation, application systems management. He does not pretend to have skills he lacks.

3. **Level flexibility** — he is genuinely willing to step down to Product Owner or Team Leader to re-enter the market, while also applying for Head of / GM level roles.

4. **Location constraints** — eliminated one role (Reo Group) purely on geography despite full skill alignment.

### The Two-Track Strategy in Practice

Simon is not searching for two separate types of roles. He is searching for **one type of role that manifests differently depending on the org structure:**

- In product-led companies: he wants to be the Product Manager/Lead who shapes what gets built
- In technology-led companies: he wants to be the Head of IT/Technology who transforms how technology serves users

**The common thread is: someone who connects user needs to technology decisions and builds the bridge between strategy and execution.**

This insight should inform how he positions himself in applications. He is not a generic PM or a generic IT Manager. He is a **technology-literate builder who creates user-centric systems** — and that framing works across both tracks.
